CT1

Pattern origin comics · "How Observer was born"

Each pattern gets a 6-panel visual comic telling the story of why it exists. Observer: PARC 1979 → MVC → GoF → React hooks. Engineers remember stories, not definitions.

1979
Xerox PARC · Trygve Reenskaug invents MVC for Smalltalk. Model needs to notify View when it changes...
1995
GoF formalize Observer: Subject + Observer interface + subscribe/unsubscribe. The name sticks.
2013
React launches. useState + useEffect = Observer in 2 lines. Pattern becomes invisible...
2020
RxJS, Signals, Svelte stores — Observer evolves into reactive. The idea expands...

CT2

Design disasters · real war stories of what happens without the pattern

Each pattern has a "war story" page. Real incidents where missing or misusing the pattern caused production outages. Knight Capital (2012, $440M loss) as an example of missing Circuit Breaker. AWS S3 outage 2017 as cascading failure without Bulkhead.

💥
Knight Capital · August 1, 2012
The $440M 45-minute trading disaster
Circuit Breaker
A deployment left 1 of 8 servers running old code with a dormant flag re-enabled. The server sent bogus orders — thousands per second. No circuit breaker stopped the cascade. By the time humans reacted, Knight Capital had lost $440M and bankrupted itself.
Lesson: Circuit Breaker isn't nice-to-have — it's the difference between "weird Tuesday" and "company ends".
12-15 patterns have iconic disaster stories. Opus writes + links to post-mortems.

CT4

Choose-your-own-design-adventure · branching story per pattern

Interactive fiction where each decision shapes the system you build. "You're the lead engineer. Product wants real-time stock prices in 50ms. Which do you reach for first? [Observer] [WebSocket + event bus] [Polling]" Each choice branches. Wrong choices reveal the consequences mid-story. Right choices unlock the pattern's nuance.

Scene 3 · Wednesday, 10 AM
Product Manager slacks: "The CFO dashboard is lagging 2 seconds behind trades. Fix before demo Friday."
You check the code. One StockPrice class pushes updates to 4 dashboards via direct method calls. Now we need 15 dashboards.
A. Add Observer — let each dashboard subscribe
B. Move it all to a Redis pub-sub bus
C. Keep the direct calls, just add 11 more
Pick C = see cascade failure at scale. Pick A = next scene: WebSocket fan-out challenges. Pick B = "you over-engineered. PM is angry. how do you defend?"

CT6

Pattern personalities · each pattern is a character

Ted Chiang / Kazuo Ishiguro-style vignettes where patterns have personalities. Observer is the anxious attention-seeker. Singleton is the lone monk. Mediator is the exhausted diplomat. Circuit Breaker is the paranoid veteran. Each gets a 100-word character sketch + memorable tagline.

🧘
Singleton
"There can only be one."
Singleton sits alone in the application, watched over by the runtime. Everyone thinks they know where to find him — and they do. But his solitude costs him: he can't be mocked, can't be tested in isolation, can't evolve without touching everyone. "Being the only one sounds like power," he says, "but it's mostly loneliness."
36 characters. 36 taglines. Ishiguro-grade prose from Opus. Patterns become memorable because they're human.
CT7

Pattern dialogues · podcast-style conversations between patterns

"Observer and Pub-Sub walk into a bar..." Two patterns debate when to use which. Written as scripts (optional audio). Observer defends itself: "I keep subs & subject in the same process." Pub-Sub pushes back: "Exactly your problem — you don't scale past one machine." Observer sulks. Pub-Sub buys beer.

🎙 THE OBSERVER / PUB-SUB DEBATE · scene 1
OBSERVER: I've served React, Angular, Vue for years. What's your deal?
PUB-SUB: I serve 15 microservices at once. You couldn't keep up past 1,000 subscribers.
OBSERVER: (sulking) I never claimed to. That's what message brokers are for.
PUB-SUB: So admit it — you're in-memory only.
OBSERVER: And you need infrastructure! Kafka. RabbitMQ. Configs. I'm 3 classes in your app.
PUB-SUB: (pauses, sips) Fair.

CT10

A week in the life · fictional engineer's design diary

Short serialized diary (Monday → Friday) of a fictional mid-level engineer navigating real design challenges. Day 1: Product wants feature X. Day 3: she reaches for Observer. Day 5: realizes she should have used Strategy. Reflective, honest, human. Written like Notes from the Architect meets The Mythical Man-Month.

WEDNESDAY · 11:47 PM
Reviewed my PR tonight. The senior engineer wrote: "This looks like Observer. Why not use an event emitter?"

I almost snapped back. I thought I had picked correctly. Observer was explicit, typed, had clear handler contracts. Event emitters are loose. Why would I want less structure?

But 2 hours of reading later... I see it. In this codebase, 5 modules will need to listen. None of them have a compile-time relationship. I was picking Observer because I was nostalgic. EventEmitter is right for this.

Pattern knowledge is a hammer. Choosing the pattern is the hard part.
